UK growth policy

How work can pay again

A practical guide to the Labour Growth Group report An Honest Day: A new economic settlement for Britain: why work feels less rewarding, why essentials cost too much, and what it would mean to fix shortages instead of subsidising them forever.

support now build supply capture uplift lower costs

This page turns the Labour Growth Group's An Honest Day: A new economic settlement for Britain into a non-specialist guide. In plain English, the report says Britain has become too good at rewarding people who control scarce things, and not good enough at rewarding people who work, build, care, invest or take useful risks.

Core argument

It is a cost-of-living plus state-capability argument

The report is not just saying "spend more" or "cut red tape". It says government should identify where scarcity is artificially created, where public money is being captured by broken systems, and where the state lacks the machinery to decide, build or enforce.

Then the state should change those systems so work, investment and useful risk-taking pay again. Support still matters, but support should be a bridge to a cheaper system, not a permanent payment into a broken one.

Translation guide

The report's key terms, made concrete

Economic settlement

The basic deal between citizens, markets and the state.

In practice: if I work hard, can I afford a home, raise a family, save, and feel the country works for me?

Extraction economy

Making money from control, scarcity or loopholes.

In practice: land rises in value because permission is scarce, or essential services carry heavy debt while bills keep rising.

Rents and rent capture

Extra profit from owning a scarce position.

In practice: a landowner gains from a new station or planning decision even if they did not build the homes or transport.

State incapability

Government cannot reliably decide, build or enforce.

In practice: grid connections take years, housing schemes stall, and local leaders are blamed while key powers sit elsewhere.

Cost of essentials

Growth depends on the basics becoming cheaper.

In practice: a pay rise does little if rent, childcare, commuting and energy bills absorb it. A factory invests less if power is too expensive.

Reward action, not position

Favour work, building and risk over passive gains.

In practice: the report argues for taxing gains more fairly while cutting the burden on work, such as employee National Insurance.

Outcomes, not process

Safeguards matter, but process should not become a veto.

In practice: for major infrastructure, decide the national interest up front, then use one route to actually build.

Multiple economic engines

Do not rely so heavily on London and the South East.

In practice: give mayors and local authorities more control over transport, housing, skills, land and local investment.

Fake-market capitalism

A market where users cannot really walk away.

In practice: water, care or dentistry may need tougher ownership, resilience and accountability rules because normal competition is weak.

Cheap clean power

Clean energy succeeds if it lowers bills.

In practice: wind farms are not enough. Power needs grid capacity, storage, backup, faster connections and market rules that cut the total bill.

Locational pricing

Prices reveal where power is cheap or scarce.

In practice: if wind is abundant in one region but the grid cannot move it, prices should expose that bottleneck so investment goes where it helps.

Anti-system majority

Voters who feel the system is rigged or unresponsive.

In practice: people drifting to different parties may still share the same complaint: basics cost too much and government cannot fix it.

The feedback loop

How a broken system keeps funding itself

1

Shortage

Planning, grid, care, transport or capital systems ration something essential.

2

Higher prices

People who already control the scarce asset or position gain bargaining power.

3

Public support

Government steps in because households and firms cannot wait for reform.

4

Capture

If supply is still fixed, some support flows into the same high prices.

5

Less room to act

The state spends more maintaining failure and has less capacity to fix it.

Worked examples

The pattern appears in housing and energy

Housing

If public decisions turn low-value land into high-value housing land, some of that uplift can pay for the transport, schools, utilities and affordable homes that make the development work.

Energy

If clean generation exists but cannot reach homes and factories cheaply, the country has not solved the cost problem. The test is useful clean electricity delivered at low total system cost.

Plan before permission

A mayor, council or strategic authority identifies a growth area, assembles land where needed, and sets a single plan for homes, transport, schools, utilities and public space.

Central government may provide grants or guarantees. Local government may borrow against future revenues. Developers pay for homes and agreed site works once the scheme is viable.

Planning permission and infrastructure raise land value. The public captures part of that rise through land purchase, development agreements, infrastructure charges or retained growth revenues.

4

Use it to lower the shortage

Captured value pays back infrastructure costs and supports affordable homes. Rent support can continue, but the long-term answer is more homes where people need them.

Who benefits?

The timing matters

Immediately

Renters who need help still receive support. The landowner can be paid above the old land value. The public sector may spend or borrow before the new homes exist.

During development

Developers earn a return for building and taking delivery risk. The public authority receives captured uplift or contributions that help fund roads, drainage, schools and transport.

After completion

Residents get more housing choice and better infrastructure. Local government gains a stronger tax base. Central government should face less pressure to keep subsidising scarcity.

Decision test

What changes inside government?

Who is asking?

Ministers, civil servants, regulators and grid planners deciding which energy projects, market rules and network upgrades should move first.

Old question

How much clean capacity have we announced?

New question

How much affordable clean electricity reaches households and productive firms, after grid, storage, backup and constraint costs?

What they do with the answer

They move money, permits and attention toward the things that cut bills in practice: grid bottlenecks, storage, faster connections, flexible demand and market rules that reduce total system cost.

More worked examples

Where else the same logic applies

Housing and energy are not special cases. The same test can be applied to childcare, social care, transport, water and the way Britain scales companies: is public policy merely compensating for failure, or is it building the capacity and rules that make the essential thing cheaper, more reliable and more productive?

Example 3 Childcare: support should create staffed places

A parent wants to work an extra day, but nursery fees eat most of the extra income. Subsidy helps immediately, but if there are not enough nursery places or staff, public money can end up chasing the same scarce places.

Increase places

Use capital grants, public buildings, planning support and childminder start-up funding to add capacity where families actually need it.

Train and keep staff

Fund apprenticeships and early years qualifications, then tie higher funding rates to pay, training and progression.

Stabilise providers

Use multi-year funding, realistic hourly rates and extra support for children with additional needs so providers do not close while demand is high.

Attach conditions

Providers receiving extra public funding should show the places they add, the funded places they offer and how staff retention improves.

Reader test: does this policy help parents pay today, and does it create more affordable, staffed childcare places tomorrow?

Example 4 Social care: buy capacity, not just crisis management

Councils spend more on adult social care, but the system remains fragile. Care homes close, staff leave, families pay large bills, and NHS beds are blocked because people cannot be discharged safely.

Build care capacity

Give councils multi-year care settlements, capacity contracts for home-care providers and support for extra-care housing and supported living.

Retain care workers

Fund training, minimum employment standards, paid travel time and progression routes from care assistant to senior and specialist roles.

Stop extraction

Use open-book accounting, resilience tests, limits on excessive debt or dividends, and step-in powers for failing critical providers.

Connect to the NHS

Pool budgets for discharge and reablement, create joint discharge teams and reward fewer delayed discharges and avoidable readmissions.

Reader test: is public money buying better care capacity, or just keeping a fragile market from collapsing?

Example 5 Transport outside London: give places the tools to grow

A person in a second city can technically take a better job, but the bus is unreliable and the commute takes too long. The local economy loses because workers cannot reach jobs and firms cannot reach workers.

Move from bids to settlements

Replace small competitive pots with long-term transport funding so mayors and councils can plan networks rather than chase one-off grants.

Let growth help pay

Use land value capture, tax increment financing, municipal borrowing and retained growth revenues to fund local infrastructure.

Plan housing and jobs together

Align transport, land, skills and housing so new homes, employers and training routes reinforce each other.

Judge access, not ribbon-cutting

Measure whether more people can reliably reach jobs, colleges, hospitals and town centres within useful journey times.

Reader test: who has the power and money to fix the commute: the place affected, or a distant department?

Example 6 Water: protect the service, not extraction

Water is a captive essential service. Customers cannot choose another pipe network, so a failing provider should not be able to extract returns while the public absorbs poor service, underinvestment or financial fragility.

Let companies fail safely

Use special administration: keep water and wastewater services running, protect workers and suppliers, but make investors absorb losses where the structure failed.

Limit fragile finance

Apply leverage limits, stress tests, dividend locks and scrutiny of related-party payments when service or resilience is weak.

Tie bills to delivery

Link bill increases to named investment projects, milestones and outcomes such as fewer leaks, sewage discharges and resilience failures.

Make regulation bite

Give regulators faster enforcement, step-in powers, public dashboards and personal accountability for repeated failure.

Reader test: if bills rise, can customers see what real-world improvement they are paying for?

Example 7 Scale-ups: help builders keep more upside in Britain

A promising UK science or AI company starts here but sells early to a US buyer because Britain has weaker late-stage capital. The invention happens here, but ownership, scale-up jobs and much of the upside move elsewhere.

Provide patient capital

Use the National Wealth Fund, British Business Bank, co-investment and fund-of-funds vehicles to crowd in private late-stage capital.

Use procurement as a customer

Create faster routes for proven startups, break large contracts into smaller lots and scale successful public sector pilots.

Commercialise research

Fund proof-of-concept work, fairer spinout terms and experienced operators who can turn lab discoveries into companies.

Build local ecosystems

Back regional clusters with labs, compute, testbeds, power connections, technical skills, transport and suitable housing.

Reader test: is government acting as a passive referee, or building the market conditions that let productive firms scale?

Sources and scope

What this page is, and is not

The examples above are simplified teaching models. They are not costed policy proposals, legal advice, or claims that the example numbers would be appropriate for a real site or project.
