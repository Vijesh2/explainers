UK electricity markets

Why gas sets electricity prices

A simple market-clearing model for the UK: cheaper power is used first, but the final generator needed to meet demand often sets the wholesale price for everyone.

wind nuclear imports gas

The puzzle is not that all electricity is made from gas. It is that the wholesale market usually clears at the price of the most expensive generator still needed. In Britain, that marginal generator is often gas.

Core mechanism

The first half: how the price is formed

1

Rank generators by running cost

Wind, solar, nuclear, and some imports usually have lower short-run operating costs than gas. The market stacks available supply from cheaper to more expensive.

2

Use enough supply to meet demand

The system accepts blocks of generation until demand is covered. The last accepted block is the marginal generator.

3

Pay the clearing price

In a pay-as-clear market, accepted generators receive the market clearing price for that period, even if some bid below it.

4

Gas often fills the gap

Gas plants are flexible and dispatchable, so they often cover the final slice when demand is high or renewable output is low.

The tempting question

Why not just change the pricing rule?

That question is worth asking, but the answer is not a single obvious fix. Different designs shift risk, investment signals, and costs in different places.

Mental model

What actually reduces gas's influence

More renewables help most when they push gas out of the marginal position. Storage, flexible demand, interconnectors, and grid upgrades all make that easier.

Long-term contracts can also reduce how much wholesale volatility reaches consumers, even while the live market still needs a price for balancing supply and demand.

The issue is not that marginal pricing is obviously wrong. It is that consumers remain exposed to gas prices while gas is still often the final generator needed.

How gas becomes rare

The scale of flexibility needed

There is no single number that makes gas disappear from pricing. The practical goal is to make gas run rarely enough that it is seldom the marginal generator. The UK Clean Power 2030 pathway is a useful benchmark: clean sources would provide at least 95% of generation in a typical year, while gas capacity is still retained for security of supply.

23-27 GW

Battery storage

Covers short evening peaks and stores surplus wind or solar for use over minutes and hours. This pushes gas out of many ordinary peak periods, but not multi-day low-renewable events.

10-12 GW

Flexible demand

Shifts EV charging, heat pumps, appliances, and some business demand away from tight periods. Lowering the peak can be as valuable as building another generator.

12-14 GW

Interconnectors

Lets Britain import when neighbours have surplus power and export when Britain has excess renewables. It helps, but neighbouring systems can also be tight at the same time.

80 projects

Grid upgrades

Moves clean power from where it is generated to where it is needed. Without network capacity, gas can still be needed because cheap renewable output is trapped behind constraints.

The hardest hours are not normal peaks.

The difficult case is a cold, high-demand, low-wind period lasting several days. That is why the 2030 pathway also includes 4-6 GW of long-duration storage and expects 40-50 GW of dispatchable and long-duration flexible capacity. Until low-carbon alternatives can cover those periods, gas may run rarely but still matter for security and occasional price-setting.

Battery buildout

Is this actually happening?

Yes. Battery storage is actively being developed by government, system operators, regulators, private developers, investors, and trading companies. The question is not whether batteries are being built, but whether enough projects can connect, finance, and commission quickly enough.

7.2 GW Operational in Q1 2026

Modo Energy reported around 7.2 GW and 11.8 GWh of operational battery storage in Great Britain by Q1 2026.

23-27 GW Clean Power 2030 target

The target is plausible, but it requires roughly another 16-20 GW of batteries by 2030.

30 GW Project pipeline

The project pipeline is large enough on paper, but grid access and delivery timing decide how much becomes real capacity.

Who is building it?

DESNZ sets the policy target, NESO manages system operation and connection reform, Ofgem regulates market access, and private developers build the assets. Developers and investors include firms such as SSE, Zenobe, Harmony Energy, Gresham House, Field, Statera, and Gore Street.

What could slow it down?

Grid connections are the main practical constraint. Battery revenues are also volatile because projects earn from several markets: wholesale trading, balancing, frequency response, and Capacity Market payments. If revenue expectations fall, some projects may stall even if they have planning approval.

What batteries can and cannot do

Today's GB batteries are mostly short-duration. They are very useful for evening peaks, fast balancing, and absorbing surplus renewables over a few hours. They do not solve a cold, still, low-wind period lasting several days. That still needs long-duration storage, demand flexibility, interconnectors, or low-carbon dispatchable power.
