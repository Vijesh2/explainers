UK gilt pricing

How to bootstrap a gilt yield curve

A full walkthrough using actual conventional gilt prices from the Tradeweb FTSE UK Gilt Closing Prices snapshot for 15 May 2026. Start from dirty prices, infer discount factors, and turn those into zero-coupon spot rates.

The current explainer plots redemption yields. Bootstrapping goes one layer deeper. It treats each gilt price as the sum of dated cash flows, then solves for the market-implied discount factor at each maturity. From those discount factors we derive zero-coupon spot rates.

Simplifications addressed

What this explainer does and does not assume

Handled explicitly

The explainer builds semi-annual coupon calendars from each maturity, computes settlement from the price date, applies ACT/ACT accrued interest, handles ex-dividend rebate interest, uses dirty prices, and discounts every remaining cash flow by date.

Chosen modelling rule

Real gilts do not all pay coupons on the same dates. When a coupon date falls between two already bootstrapped maturities, this explainer interpolates the log discount factor between those solved dates. That choice is visible in the calculations below.

Still simplified

The explainer uses weekends rather than a full UK bank-holiday calendar, uses a small short-end sample, and does not run instrument filters for liquidity, tax distortions, special collateral effects, or outlier prices.

Production version

A production curve would usually fit many instruments at once or bootstrap with a formal interpolation and smoothing policy. The point here is to make the arithmetic inspectable.

Equation

Each dirty price is a sum of discounted cash flows

Dirty price = Σ cash flow on date t × discount factor for date t

Spot rate for maturity T = discount factor(T)-1/T - 1
