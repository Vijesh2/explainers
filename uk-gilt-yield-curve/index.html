UK gilt pricing

How to build
a yield curve

This explainer shows how to build a yield curve from market prices of gilts: move from quoted clean prices to dirty settlement prices, solve each gilt's yield, then line those yields up by maturity.

The market quotes gilts clean so the price does not jump every day as coupon interest accrues. The buyer pays dirty: clean price plus accrued interest. Yield curve construction starts from that dirty price because discounted cash-flow maths needs the full cash settlement amount. The worked example below uses a Tradeweb FTSE UK Gilt Closing Prices snapshot for conventional gilts, close of business 15 May 2026, with prices shown per £100 nominal.

Step 3

Apply day count, ex-dividend, and settlement rules

Settlement date

Normal gilt-market settlement is T+1. The 15 May 2026 close was a Friday, so the next business day settlement date is 18 May 2026. Accrued interest and ex-dividend entitlement are based on settlement date, not the trade date.

ACT/ACT accrued interest

For a conventional gilt outside the ex-dividend period, accrued interest equals the next semi-annual coupon multiplied by actual days from the previous coupon date to settlement, divided by actual days in the coupon period.

Ex-dividend treatment

If settlement falls after the ex-dividend date, the buyer will not receive the next coupon. Accrued interest turns negative, often called rebate interest, and is deducted from the clean price.

For the selected gilt: accrued interest = coupon/2 × elapsed days ÷ days in coupon period.

Plot the yield against maturity

Step 5

What the calculation is doing

1

Use the dirty price

A discounted cash-flow equation values every future coupon and the final £100 principal. The value must equal the amount paid, so clean price alone is incomplete.

2

Build the cash-flow schedule

Conventional gilts pay fixed coupons semi-annually. For each bond, list every remaining coupon date, then add the final £100 redemption payment to the maturity-date cash flow.

3

Solve the yield

Find the single annual rate that makes the present value of those cash flows equal the dirty price. That is the redemption yield for this one gilt.

4

Fit or bootstrap the curve

A simple explainer can connect redemption yields. A production curve usually estimates zero-coupon discount factors first, then derives spot and forward rates.

Caveats

A market curve needs more than a line chart

This page uses the attached Tradeweb FTSE closing-price snapshot to teach the mechanics for conventional gilts as of 15 May 2026. The worked example applies semi-annual coupon schedules, T+1 settlement, ACT/ACT accrued interest, seven-business-day ex-dividend dates, and negative rebate interest inside the ex-dividend period. It still uses a simplified yield solve: official production curves also need full holiday calendars, exact market rounding, instrument filters, and the chosen curve-fitting or bootstrapping method. Index-linked gilts are excluded because their cash flows depend on RPI indexation.
