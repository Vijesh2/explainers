UK public finance

How the gilt market works

Start with one bond: coupons, price, yield, and maturity. Then zoom out to the UK government bond market, where gilt yields shape the cost of funding public spending.

coupon price yield issuance debt interest

A gilt is a sterling liability of the UK government. Investors lend money today, receive scheduled payments, and expect the principal back at maturity. The market price of those promises moves every day, and the yield implied by that price becomes a benchmark for the government's cost of borrowing.

Mechanism

Price and yield move in opposite directions

1

The coupon is set when the gilt is issued

A 3.5% conventional gilt pays £3.50 a year for every £100 of nominal value, normally in two half-yearly payments.

2

The price changes in the secondary market

If investors require a higher return, they pay less for the same fixed cash flows. That lower price pushes the yield up.

3

New borrowing reflects market yields

The DMO sells new gilts through auctions and syndications. Higher market yields mean the government must offer investors a higher expected return on new or refinanced debt.

4

Index-linked gilts add inflation exposure

Index-linked gilts adjust coupons and principal with RPI, so inflation shocks can move debt interest costs faster than with fixed coupons.

From bond to market

How annual borrowing becomes gilt supply

1. Cash need

The government must finance new cash borrowing and refinance maturing gilts. For 2026-27 the Debt Management Report put the gross financing requirement at £273.4bn.

2. Financing plan

NS&I, Treasury bills, and other items cover part of the need. The DMO planned £252.1bn of gilt sales for debt financing in 2026-27.

3. Maturity mix

Issuance is split across short, medium, long, index-linked, and unallocated gilts to balance cost, refinancing risk, demand, and market liquidity.

Scale
